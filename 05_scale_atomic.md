# Worked Case: Hydrogen and Helium

## Purpose

This is a concrete demonstration of the framework operating at the atomic scale — not a description of what the framework claims, but the framework doing actual explanatory work on a specific system. Hydrogen first, because it is the simplest possible case. Helium second, because it is where Pauli exclusion becomes visible and consequential.

---

## Case 1: Hydrogen

### The system

One proton. One electron. Nothing else.

### Step 1: The constraint geometry

The proton carries positive charge. The electron carries negative charge. Between them, the electromagnetic field defines a constraint landscape — a potential energy that varies with distance:

> V(r) = −e²/4πε₀r

This is not a force pulling two separate objects together. It is the shape of the constraint geometry: the closer the electron is to the proton, the deeper it sits in the potential well. The geometry defines a landscape with a minimum — a region where the configuration is most stable — and rising potential in all directions away from it.

The electron cannot simply sit at the bottom of this well. It is a quantum object — it has wavelike behavior that prevents precise localization. Confining it more tightly increases its kinetic energy. So the stable configuration is not "as close as possible" but the standing wave pattern that balances the pull of the electromagnetic well against the cost of confinement.

This is the first demonstration of the framework's core claim operating concretely: the system does not explore all configurations equally. The constraint geometry — electromagnetic attraction plus the quantum cost of confinement — selects for specific stable forms.

### Step 2: Physically realizable states

The Schrödinger equation applied to this constraint geometry yields a discrete set of solutions — the orbitals. These are the physically realizable states: standing wave patterns where the electron's configuration is self-consistent under the constraints acting on it.

The ground state — the most stable configuration — is the 1s orbital:

> ψ₁ₛ(r) ∝ e^(−r/a₀)

where a₀ is the Bohr radius (~0.53 Å), the characteristic length scale at which the electromagnetic constraint and the quantum cost of confinement reach their lowest combined value.

This is not a circular orbit. It is a spherically symmetric probability distribution — a cloud of amplitude centered on the proton, falling off exponentially with distance. The electron is most likely to be found near the nucleus but has nonzero probability of being found at any distance. There is no sharp boundary.

**In framework terms**: the 1s orbital is a persistent, boundary-maintaining configuration. It is the standing wave pattern the constraint geometry selects as most stable. The "boundary" of the atom is not a line but a region where the probability amplitude falls below functional significance — the framework's statistical boundary definition operating precisely.

### Step 3: The boundary

Where does hydrogen end?

Not at a fixed radius. The probability amplitude decays exponentially but never reaches exactly zero. The boundary is a transition differential — the region where the electron's interaction probability with external systems drops sharply enough to define a functionally distinct inside and outside.

At approximately 1–2 Bohr radii, the electron density has fallen enough that chemical interactions with other atoms become the relevant constraint. The boundary of hydrogen is the region where the internal constraint geometry gives way to the geometry of interaction with other systems. It is maintained by the electromagnetic constraint landscape, not drawn by an observer.

**In framework terms**: hydrogen is a self-maintaining constraint configuration. Its boundary exists because the geometry that sustains it — electromagnetic attraction balanced against quantum confinement cost — produces a sharp enough transition differential between inside and outside to constitute a persistent, distinguishable region.

### Step 4: Excited states and transitions

The Schrödinger equation yields not just the ground state but a hierarchy of excited states — 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 3d, and so on — each a stable standing wave solution at higher energy.

These are also physically realizable states, but they are less stable than the ground state. A hydrogen atom in an excited state will, given sufficient time, transition to the ground state, releasing the energy difference as a photon.

**In framework terms**: the excited states are metastable configurations — standing wave patterns the geometry permits but does not favor. The transition to ground state is the geometry finding its lowest stable configuration. The emitted photon is the released constraint energy — the difference between two stable configurations of the same propagating geometry.

## Case 2: Helium

### The system

Two protons (plus two neutrons) in the nucleus. Two electrons.

### Step 1: The constraint geometry

The nucleus now carries charge +2. Each electron experiences stronger electromagnetic attraction than in hydrogen. But now there is an additional constraint: the two electrons repel each other.

The constraint geometry is no longer spherically symmetric around a single center. It is shaped by three simultaneous interactions: nucleus-electron 1, nucleus-electron 2, and electron 1-electron 2. The system must find configurations that are stable under all three simultaneously.

### Step 2: Pauli exclusion operating concretely

Both electrons are fermions. The combined wavefunction must be antisymmetric under exchange — if you swap the two electrons, the wavefunction flips sign.

Now ask: can both electrons occupy the 1s orbital?

The 1s orbital describes spatial configuration. Two electrons in the same spatial orbital with the same spin would be identical — exchanging them would leave the wavefunction unchanged. But the antisymmetry requirement says it must flip sign. The only resolution:

> ψ = −ψ → ψ = 0

The configuration has zero amplitude. It is not physically realizable.

But two electrons in the same spatial orbital with *opposite* spins are not identical — they differ in spin quantum number. Exchanging them produces the required sign flip while remaining nonzero. This configuration *is* physically realizable.

**This is Pauli exclusion operating concretely**: both electrons occupy the 1s orbital, but they must have opposite spins. The orbital holds exactly two electrons — one spin-up, one spin-down — and no more. The constraint is not a rule imposed from outside. It is the internal symmetry structure of the geometry forcing the wavefunction to zero for any configuration that would violate antisymmetry.

**In framework terms**: the Pauli exclusion principle is the symmetry geometry of S assigning zero amplitude to configurations that are not physically realizable under the antisymmetry condition. The 1s orbital being "full" is the constraint geometry having selected all available stable configurations at that energy level. No room remains — not because of crowding, but because every configuration that would add a third electron to the 1s orbital has zero amplitude by internal structure.

### Step 3: Why helium is inert

With both electrons in the 1s orbital — opposite spins, ground state — helium's constraint geometry is closed. The 1s orbital is filled. The next available orbital, 2s, is significantly higher in energy. To interact chemically with another atom, helium would need to either share one of its 1s electrons or accept an additional electron into the 2s orbital.

Sharing a 1s electron would destabilize the closed configuration — the remaining electron would no longer experience the full electromagnetic constraint of the nucleus screened by a partner, and the energy cost is high. Accepting an additional electron into 2s is also energetically costly — the 1s electrons partially screen the nucleus, and the 2s orbital experiences weaker attraction.

The result: helium has no energetically favorable pathway to chemical interaction. Its constraint geometry is self-sufficient — a closed standing wave configuration with no available high-rate transitions to external systems.

**In framework terms**: helium is a maximally self-contained persistent configuration. Its boundary-maintenance behavior requires no interaction with external constraint geometries. This is the framework's boundary concept at its clearest — not just a statistical transition differential, but a configuration where the internal geometry is so stable and complete that external interaction would only lower transition rates within the system. The boundary is maintained not by active exclusion but by the absence of high-rate crossing paths.

### Step 4: The contrast with hydrogen

Hydrogen has one electron in the 1s orbital. The orbital can hold two. There is an available high-rate transition: sharing an electron with another atom (or accepting one) to fill the 1s orbital and reach a helium-like closed configuration. This is exactly what hydrogen does in chemical bonding — it moves toward the closed, stable configuration its constraint geometry makes accessible.

Helium has already reached that configuration. Hydrogen is a constraint geometry with an available high-rate transition to a more stable configuration. Helium is a constraint geometry with none.

**In framework terms**: chemical reactivity is the availability of high-rate transitions to more stable constraint configurations. Inertness is the absence of such transitions — a configuration that is already at or near its geometric minimum under the constraints acting on it.

## What the Framework Has Demonstrated

Working through hydrogen and helium concretely, the framework has shown:

**Boundary as statistical transition differential**: the edge of an atom is not a line but a region where probability amplitude decays and interaction likelihood drops — exactly the framework's definition, now with a precise mechanism.

**Persistence as standing wave stability**: orbitals are not arbitrary — they are the configurations the constraint geometry selects as self-consistent. The ground state is the most stable because it minimizes the combined cost of electromagnetic attraction and quantum confinement.

**Pauli exclusion as internal geometry**: the filled orbital is not a rule imposed from outside but a consequence of the antisymmetry structure of the state space. Zero-amplitude configurations are not removed from the mathematical space — they are rendered physically inert by internal symmetry.

**Reactivity as constraint geometry seeking stability**: hydrogen reacts because its geometry has available high-rate transitions to more stable configurations. Helium does not because its geometry is already closed. Chemical behavior follows directly from constraint geometry without requiring additional explanation.

**The framework's limits here**: the helium calculation is exact only in principle. In practice, the electron-electron repulsion makes the two-electron Schrödinger equation analytically unsolvable — approximation methods are required. This is the first appearance of the complexity problem that becomes dominant at larger scales: even two interacting electrons require approximation. The exact geometry remains, but direct resolution of it requires coarse-graining even here.

## The Bridge Upward

Hydrogen and helium are the framework operating in its cleanest regime — few particles, exact symmetry, analytically tractable (or nearly so). The next scale — molecules, then larger atoms with filled shells, then biological molecules — introduces more electrons, more nuclei, and more simultaneous constraint interactions.

The geometry remains exact. The resolution required to describe it directly increases beyond what is tractable. Decoherence and coarse-graining are not yet relevant at the molecular scale — quantum chemistry handles molecules quantum mechanically. But complexity is already forcing approximation at two electrons.

The upward extension of the framework will follow this thread: not geometry relaxing, but direct resolution becoming increasingly intractable as constraint interactions multiply — until the scale at which statistical description becomes not just convenient but necessary.

## Added Formal Notes: Pauli and Quantum Boundary Types

### Pauli Exclusion Derivation (Antisymmetry)

For two identical fermions, the exchange symmetry condition is:

\[
\psi(x_1, x_2) = -\psi(x_2, x_1)
\]

If two electrons are forced into the same full quantum state, swapping labels leaves the state unchanged, which requires \(\psi = -\psi\), so \(\psi = 0\). This is the exclusion result: those configurations are structurally disallowed by antisymmetry.

### Quantum Boundary Types

| Boundary type | Definition | Crossable? | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Symmetry boundary | Antisymmetry implies \(\psi = 0\) for forbidden configurations | No | Pauli exclusion |
| Energetic boundary | High transition cost / low transition rate in the Hamiltonian landscape | Yes, probabilistically | Potential barrier with tunneling |

This distinction clarifies why some boundaries are exact structural exclusions while others are probabilistic barriers.
